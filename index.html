Leo XIII opened his pontificate not with a program but with a diagnosis. The expulsion of God from civil society, he argued, does not produce a neutral public square. It produces a public square ordered toward whatever fills the vacuum — toward appetite, toward power, toward the interests of those with sufficient resources to direct the institutions that remain. The 147 years since have documented this diagnosis in categories Leo XIII could not have named but whose logic he had already described.

The legal order is the most consequential domain of this documentation. When the Supreme Court declared in Roe v. Wade that a living human organism was not a person for constitutional purposes, it did not merely authorize a medical procedure. It established as the governing principle of the Republic that personhood is a grant of the sovereign will rather than a recognition of a pre-political natural reality. This principle cannot be quarantined to reproductive law. It is the premise that makes every subsequent act of the sovereign will immune to challenge on grounds of natural justice.

The 63 million deaths that followed are not an unintended consequence. They are the principle in operation. They are also the demographic foundation of every fiscal crisis documented in the remainder of this index. The missing generation appears in the projections not as a moral category but as a variable — a shortfall in workers, in taxpayers, in parents, in citizens. The variable has a cause. The cause has a name.

The opioid crisis documents the same pattern at a different scale. A population that had lost the transcendent framework within which suffering makes sense — the religious community, the stable family, the civic belonging that once gave ordinary American life its moorings — proved catastrophically susceptible to a pharmaceutical product marketed with misrepresented safety claims through a regulatory apparatus that the pharmaceutical industry had helped shape. The susceptibility was not created by the pharmaceutical companies. It was created by decades of dismantling the structures that Leo XIII identified as the necessary conditions of a civilization ordered toward the good of its members.

The natural right to private property, in Leo XIII's framework, derives from the nature of the human person as a rational agent capable of providing for himself and his family across time. Remove the property and you remove the stake. Remove the stake and you remove the civic investment that makes self-governance possible. Remove the reasonable expectation that effort produces outcome and you remove something deeper still: the interior conviction that human action matters — that the future is something one builds rather than something that happens to one.

Both removals have occurred in the United States simultaneously. The private equity transformation of the American economy has concentrated productive property upward through financial mechanism. Between 2010 and 2023, institutional investors acquired single-family homes at a scale without precedent, competing against first-time homeowners at a scale no individual can match. A generation directed toward credentialed professional employment borrowed $1.77 trillion for credentials now being deprecated by artificial intelligence. The debt remains. The career the debt was supposed to purchase has had its entry point removed.

The interior consequence is what the Gallup data measures. Twenty-seven million fewer Americans thriving. Nineteen percent of the global workforce fully engaged. Twenty-three percent of Gen Z reporting excellent mental health. These numbers describe a population that has lost — through accumulating economic barriers, technological displacement, and the removal of the frameworks that once gave ordinary work its dignity — the specific conviction Leo XIII identified as the necessary interior condition of a free civilization: the belief that human effort connects to human outcome.

The lobbying and campaign finance data establishes the mechanism by which these conditions are maintained rather than addressed. An industry that spends $4.7 billion annually on federal lobbying does so because the return exceeds the return available through productive investment. The social programs that nominally address the consequences of the extraction are themselves riddled with documented fraud that the legislature declines to address because the fraud flows to constituencies whose votes it needs and the complexity sustains industries whose lobbying reinforces the complexity. The worker is extracted from above, displaced from the middle, and served below by a system that consumes a documented fraction of every dollar allocated to his assistance before it reaches him.

The information ecosystem is infrastructure — the same way roads and water systems are infrastructure — because a functioning democracy requires a shared factual baseline from which collective problem-solving can proceed. The collapse of that baseline is not primarily a social media story. It is a structural story about ownership concentration, incentive misalignment, and the systematic elimination of the accountability journalism layer that historically connected local communities to the facts of their own governance.

In 1983, 50 companies controlled the majority of US media. By 2000 that number had contracted to 6. That consolidation has continued. The formal protections of a free press remain. The operational incentives of an advertising business — which structurally favor conflict, fear, and tribal identity over accuracy, complexity, and accountability — have replaced the editorial mission that once made those protections meaningful.

The internal research at major social platforms — documented in congressional testimony and investigative reporting — shows that recommendation algorithms select for the most emotionally activating content regardless of its truth value. The platforms' own researchers identified this dynamic, proposed corrections, and were overruled by business units. Emotional activation drives engagement. Engagement drives time on platform. Time on platform drives advertising revenue. Accuracy is not a metric in this equation.

You cannot govern a democracy without a shared information commons. That commons no longer exists at the required scale. This is not a mood. It is a structural condition with measurable political consequences — the inability to form the shared factual baseline from which collective response to collective problems would need to proceed.

The demographic-fiscal trap is the slow-motion catastrophe that makes every other number in this index worse. Social Security and Medicare were designed for a demographic reality in which 5+ workers supported each retiree. That reality has not existed for decades. The ratio stands at 2.7 today and continues falling. The actuarial math is not a projection or a model. It is arithmetic applied to conditions that have not changed and are not projected to change.

The family formation crisis is both a demographic problem and a social capital problem. Marriage rates and birth rates are at historic lows simultaneously. Polling consistently shows young Americans desire children at rates higher than they are having them — the gap between desired and actual fertility is filled with documented economic barriers: housing unaffordability, student debt, wage stagnation, and the collapse of the cultural and religious frameworks that historically supported family formation. The barriers are not natural. They are the accumulated consequence of policy choices whose connection to the demographic outcome has not been honestly examined in public discourse.

The criminological case is separate from the demographic case but traces to the same cause. The research on fatherlessness and violent crime is extensive, consistent, and politically inconvenient across the spectrum. Boys raised without fathers are between two and three times more likely to commit violent crimes. Communities with high rates of single-parent households experience violent crime rates that persist across economic cycles and geographic variables. The family is not merely a social preference. It is, as Leo XIII argued in 1880, the institution upon which civil order rests — and the violent crime data documents what happens to civil order when that institution is treated as optional.

Social capital — the connective tissue that allows communities to respond to crises without routing everything through centralized institutions — has hollowed out alongside the family. The civic associations, fraternal organizations, religious communities, and service clubs that once provided the infrastructure for decentralized collective action have declined in parallel with marriage rates and religious participation. No government program has replicated any of these functions at scale. The Surgeon General's declaration of a loneliness epidemic is not a cultural observation. It is a public health finding with documented mortality consequences equivalent to smoking 15 cigarettes per day.

Infrastructure decay is the physical manifestation of a political system that systematically discounts long-term collective investment in favor of short-term political incentives. Roads, bridges, water systems, the electrical grid, and wastewater treatment do not produce ribbon-cutting moments on the maintenance schedule. They produce catastrophic failures — collapses, contamination events, cascading blackouts — when the maintenance that was deferred long enough finally comes due. The political incentive structure does not reward maintenance. It rewards construction. And so maintenance is deferred until the deferral becomes visible catastrophe.

The fiscal interaction compounds this. The infrastructure deficit requires $2.6 trillion in investment over ten years according to ASCE. The national debt trajectory — $1 trillion in annual interest payments growing to $2.1 trillion by 2036 — removes the fiscal space that would be required to address this deficit. The physical infrastructure of the country will continue to deteriorate at a pace determined by the rate at which current facilities fail, not by any rational maintenance or investment plan. This is not a projection. It is the current trajectory absent a change in fiscal conditions that no administration of either party has proposed at the required scale.

Leo XIII in 1881 identified the governing principle: political authority that is not ordered toward the common good does not become neutral. It becomes ordered toward the interests of those who hold it — which is to say, toward the short-term, toward the visible, toward the ribbon-cutting and the press release. The common good requires stewardship across time horizons that exceed the electoral cycle. Infrastructure is the most concrete expression of this requirement. The C− grade is the most concrete expression of the failure to meet it.

The term remains in use. The concept has been abandoned. The workers whose dignity it was introduced to defend carry the debt of the misdirection that abandoned it.

The verdict
Leo XIII wrote these nine encyclicals between 1878 and 1891. He was not predicting. He was diagnosing — identifying the prior conditions whose consequences would unfold according to their own logic across whatever time remained.

The prior condition was singular: a civilization removing God from its legal and institutional order and believing it could sustain the goods that order had produced without the foundation that had produced them. The goods were real — the rule of law, human dignity, family stability, the care of the poor, the common good. The foundation was what Leo XIII spent thirteen years articulating: that these goods are not self-sustaining. They derive from a moral order above the sovereign will, grounded in natural law, illuminated by the Church, and held accountable to a standard no government has the authority to revoke.

Remove the foundation and you do not remove the goods immediately. You remove the conditions for their continued existence. The goods persist for a time on the momentum of the culture that produced them. Then they begin to erode. Then the erosion compounds. Then the compounding reaches a scale at which the data becomes impossible to misread.

The data above is 147 years of that compounding. Nine encyclicals. Nine domains. The hypothesis was written in 1878. The proof has been assembling ever since.

The question the data cannot answer is whether the civilization retains the capacity, and the will, to read it honestly — and whether that capacity arrives before the consequences of not reading it become irreversible.
